### *i.*

Under white light, skin is hot to the touch, if you let yourself touch it. Gloves on: _nurse, hold her down_; you press a mask over Specimen 4712’s mouth and nose until she stops writhing under your grip. Open wide! Arms and legs out - _like a starfish_, they tell you in training. Her long hair hangs off the table, a tripping hazard – _clippers, nurse; this needs to be dealt with_. It runs like silk through your fingers, but your gloves dull the sensation.

_How old is she, nurse? Twenty-three? And a virgin, really?_ She’s hardly unattractive. But it’s easy to double check – _the probe, nurse;_ you push it up between her legs, onetwothree _beep!_ remove. Reading: PURE. You stand corrected. 

_Clean gloves, doctor._ Hygiene is the top priority when dealing with specimens of this quality.

You are exceptionally proud of what you do; you feel lucky to have made it this far. You would never have dreamed, back in medical school, that you could become an instrument in such a fantastic machine. No one is pleased, naturally. Journalist after journalist takes your occupation apart piece by piece: the right complain that it’s too slow and expensive when the birth rate is halving annually; the left whine that you’re exploiting the bodies of women who have already gone through enough, as if you would do this job if that were true. No, only those deemed psychologically capable make it through to you. Many volunteer; many are men. The ignorant lies of the general public make it hard to look your family in the eye at times. As if you’re doing anything other than what you’re told.

_Take her pulse, nurse._ One beat, two beat… You pinch along her arms for veins. Needle, nurse? You fill one, two, three vials with blood, smooth and red; nurse scribbles 4712 on each and sends them down the conveyor belt through a plastic curtain. You flick through the specimen’s file. _The gynaecologists haven’t seen a uterus that works this well in years,_ you report. You draw two lines, one down the centre of her body, another across her abdomen. Specimens often need their breasts removed, but that’s unlikely to be an issue here. She’s almost too perfect. _But doctor, look_ – nurse points to one of the X-rays. Broken toe; she must be a real one. The two of you are adamant that you’d be able to tell if you got sent a prototype, no matter how indistinguishable they are meant to be from the real thing. Indeed, the more you examine her, the more tell-tale signs you find. Her face is pockmarked, and there are deliberate scars tucked away on her arms. The muscles in her legs are more well-developed than in her arms. You wonder if she liked to cycle.

_Bzzt!_ The light over the curtain flashes on, and small incubators, labelled and ready, shuffle up the conveyor belt. Nurse lines them up next to the operating table, as you change your gloves.

Down to business.

From overhead, you pull down a shelf of ergonomically perfected knives and scissors. At this stage, she is still mostly flesh and none can go to waste. 

Her body opens for you, smoothly, bloodlessly.

You glance over her exposed bones with a scientist’s eye. Her lungs slowly expand, then quickly contract. _Focus on the lungs_, they tell you in training; _they’re the canary in the coal mine, the essential cog in the machine._ No matter what you are doing, you keep an eye trained on her lungs; you make sure that her heart, buried between them, pulses oneTWO, oneTWO.

Nurse opens the first box. _Ready, doctor._ With a minute pair of sharp scissors, you slowly, slowly snip away at the specimen’s liver. It quivers, as if shocked, then relaxes into your gloved hand. Snip. Snip. Each snip makes it jump. The urge is to comfort it like a bairn, but they train this one out of you. You are not emotionless, contrary to popular belief.

_Ready, nurse._ Nurse opens the incubator and you pry the shaking organ free, lowering it into its new bed. False arteries replace your gloved embrace, and the liver settles, calmed by the blood pumping through it again, oneTWO, oneTWO.

The specimen’s new organs lie next to her body on the table. They are ugly, mechanical things, a little Victorian in the way they shudder and click until connected to a specimen’s veins. Then, joined to arteries and tubes, they smooth over; the blood that initially recoiled settles in; the stomach expands, elastic; the pancreas chunders with bile; the kidneys melt, almost liquid. Her new liver slots neatly into the intricate puzzle of her body. You tenderly move, remove, leave undisturbed. The artistry involved in rebuilding a body is one of the reasons you love this job.

The womb is always a tricky one to get loose; this part is a careful game of unravelling the parts of a woman’s body designed to be protected. Snip. Gently, snip. It sits, warm and supple, in your gloved palms. You lower it into the incubator and clicking softly, the box’s metal veins warm it through. _Measurements, nurse?_ Too big. What a shame. Nurse locks the incubator and pushes it away through the curtain. You pick a dummy womb from your tray and nestle it in the hole.

Contrary to popular belief, this process is not disabling. She will still be able to have sex if she survives, if she really wants to.

You do not replace the heart and lungs at once. No, much too dangerous – the lungs are replaced much further down the line, one of the final parts of her non-mechanical body to go. _How is her heartbeat, nurse?_ Stable. _Her breathing?_ Stable; her lungs slowly expand, and quickly contract. Excellent. _Prepare to cut the sternum, nurse._ With a small saw, you make a clean slice. Nurse sends the bone down the conveyor belt in an airtight bag. Its mechanical replacement waits, but you don’t need it just yet.

With the smallest, most delicate incisions of the surgery, you unbury the heart from its pericardial coffin. It is just too big to sit neatly in your hands, muscular and tough. _Ready, nurse?_

Her lungs shudder, as if cold.

You freeze. The urge is for one’s grip to tighten, to hold with more assurance, but that is trained out of you – you can’t damage her; she’s much too valuable. This is common. For the fragile lungs to react or recoil as though bothered is common. You know this, but it has never happened to you before. Here, your training instructs, you ask nurse to check that the specimen is still unconscious, that her blood is still flowing, that the room is not too cold - everything you are supposed to do for the good of the specimen.

You don’t do any of this. Instead, with your gloved fingers wrapped around her heart, you look her in the eye.

She looks back.

She has brown eyes, almost black under the white light, her tiny pupils darting around your masked face. Overcome with the sudden urge to touch the familiar curve of her face, you swallow. Her heart beats faster in your hands.

With a dry mouth, you ask the nurse to check the dose of anaesthetic. _Normal, doctor._ You can’t take your eyes away. Terrified, she blinks back at you.

She licks her lips. Your heart jumps. She murmurs, “Please.”

You are cold.

Her heart pulses, pulses, pulses, under your grip, oneTWO oneTWO oneTWO oneTWO – “Please,” she whispers, again.

She grabs your wrist. You yelp. Her skin, unseamed by your gloved hand – her skin is against your exposed wrist; it sends shock up your arm, bile up your throat.

Nurse leaps to the task: pries you free; doctor, let go, step back, take five. Change your gloves.

But she grabs you again, her grip like steel. You are going to vomit; your fingers are numb.

She screams. _“Please!”_

Your gloves are soaked in her blood. A drop trickles down your wrist, inching towards your palm. She shuts her eyes. Her ribcage shudders with sobs.

_Get a needle, nurse._ Your voice sounds flat to your ears.

Nurse is hesitant. Is this so severe?

You find it in your spare hand anyway. You don’t remember it being put there. _Vein, nurse._

_Here, doctor._

You wish you weren’t looking at her face. Her pain folds itself in her expression as her eyes squeeze shut and she lets out a cry. This is the most humane option, you remind yourself. She can’t go through with this any more.

“Please,” she sobs, as her heart falls limp in your hand, as the pounding in your eardrums subsides.

You are only doing what you’re told.

### *ii.*

You see her everywhere, without meaning to. In newspapers, on television, in cinemas. She pops up on your laptop when you click on the wrong website and haunts you on Instagram before the moderators remember she’s too obscene for you to see. She’s on T-shirts, on album covers, in art galleries, on the front of books; she’s in black-and-white photographs stacked in piles at the back of the antique shop.

You wonder if you’re looking for her, without meaning to. You like looking at her. She’s beautiful, though the sight of her makes you feel sick. Do you want her, or do you want to be her? Her body is palpable under your fingers; you fantasise about the feeling of skin on skin as you hold her, still, in your gaze. She poses for you, never looking up, always taking pleasure in being looked at. You contain her, like this. She gives her body to you, like this.

But you are like many others who grew up with the idea of her burnt into your eyes. You played at being a model as a child; you posed in front of your mirror as a teenager, watching, waiting to become her. You are still waiting. Draped in glorious fashions, you lounge, poised, wondering if you are being looked at the way the camera looks at her. Though this isn’t really what you want. You want to look at yourself the way you look at her, through a camera, through a paintbrush, through the eyes of someone who wants nothing from you but your body. You can give it to yourself, like this. It can start to feel like yours again, like this.

She haunts you, nameless, dissected by your wandering eye.

### *iii.*

Duty calls.

Specimen 4713 is elderly. She is friendly and chatty, laughing that she is excited to greet death as a friend. She holds your gloved hands and looks you dead in the eye as you put the mask to her mouth and her body falls limp. Your hands shake as you spread her limbs on the slab. The nurse shaves her wispy dry hair. You take a reading, but you already know the answer: CONTAMINATED. Yes. She told you, before she went under, that she bore children.

_Nurse, take her pulse. Can you take her blood, too?_ You bury your disgust as the needle wrestles into her rough skin. You change your gloves, wishing they covered more of your body.

The surgery you perform is a little different for those who choose to die this early in the process. They must be post-menopausal. It is always a disappointment when the selected specimen is no longer fertile, but you cannot be seen to discriminate – there is plenty to learn regardless.

You still draw on her wizened body. You still quarter it with a knife. Her organs are limp, her bones brittle and colourless. You watch her lungs change size, captivated by the beat of her heart, oneTWO, oneTWO, its finality, your violence.

_Incubators, nurse._ You have to empty her body swiftly; the moment you remove part of her, the rest begins to die. Snip. Box. Next. Snip, next. Snip, next, next, next. You change your gloves. You change your gloves. You change your gloves, but it will never be enough; you will never be able to change your contaminated, bloody skin.

The carcass coughs as you remove its lungs. They deflate, slowly, under the weight of your touch.
